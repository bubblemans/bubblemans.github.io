Be strict to yourself, not others

Think of it as a great opportunity to learn. Moreover, everyone has different backgrounds and skillsets, which means they might think differently from you do. I usually have a really high standard on myself, and from time to time, I accidentally use the same standard on others. And it is not right. I realize that everyone is different, and it is the reason that we are able to come up with innovative ideas to solve problems. In addition, any of them might be your co-workers and boss in the future, and you‚Äôll never know. Be kind and friendly to all of them. One day, you might need helps from them.

Yes is not yes, will do is not will do

It is very common to see people answer ‚Äúyes‚Äù, ‚Äúwill do‚Äù, or ‚Äúlet‚Äôs do it‚Äù when others ask them ‚ÄúCan you do this or that?‚Äù However, that does not mean one will ensure the work is done by the deadline. I notice that people tend to answer ‚Äúyes‚Äù without a second thought. And this happens in three of my projects.

In the database project, we only have four weeks, and one of our teammates does not even answer the email, so there are only two people who can be used on the team including me. I list out some action items and discuss them with the other teammate who has a full-time internship (yes, full-time. yes during the semester), and we make some task arrangements in the first and the second meetings. Because he has an internship, he gets fewer tasks to do, and he says ‚Äúyes, will do.‚Äù His first task is to download and wrangle the data to fit into our design, and my job is to configure the cluster first. After one day, he does not finish the tasks, and I step in again and implement all the queries. We were in a rush because we started our project a little too late. Hence, I had to push him harder so that we could finish the project by the deadline. If I did not do anything, we would not be able to submit the project.

In phase 2 of the network project, on the day before a big submission, the other three teammates ask me why they can‚Äôt connect to VPN, which is the first thing to do to start on the project; and before that, I can‚Äôt remember how many ‚Äúyes‚Äù and ‚Äúlet‚Äôs do it‚Äù in our group chat when we discuss what to do and when to accomplish, but in reality, they never ssh into our VM until the last day before the deadline.

When I work on the software project, there is a weekly meeting every Friday, and we need to share some updates, discuss what to do for the next week, and solve the problems or confusion we have during the process. As a leader, I prepare a plan for everyone based on their preference and situation that we discuss before each meeting, and we discuss the viability of the plan and make modifications in the meeting. However, there are one to two people on the team who are not able to evaluate their own workload and situation in the coming week. I thought some of them were just special cases until this happened to so many people. Hence, my conclusion is to be conservative to people‚Äôs promises and be aware that they might start working on the task at the end of the timeline.

Always plan ahead, but be agile

When you realize that people might not be able to stick to their words, and plans will always change, you should be agile. This happens to my software project where we constantly change our plan, and even though we have a plan A beforehand, we still need a backup or plan B.

For example, I knew that person would not finish the log-in page in time even though he said he would, and we were not able to implement more fancy features or CSS things, so we just needed to meet the minimum requirements (log in). And because he needed more time just to create a static page, he would not able to work on the next task and follow the original plan; hence, we needed to cut down some of the features so that we could have something to demo before the presentation.

Being a leader is so so so hard

I am the leader among all three projects, and not only I need to manage and finish the project, but I also need to convince and make my teammates‚Äô lives easier. But it is so so so hard!

In the first or the second meetings, usually, we sit down and read the instructions and requirements. Sometimes, they are clear and specific, but sometimes, they are vague. What I do is making a plan that describes each milestone and task, and if everything is vague, I will read the learning objectives because instructors always design their courses based on the learning objectives. Mostly, instructors deploy these assignments to prepare us to be software engineers, so they usually try to simulate what we need to do in the industry. People always appreciate someone steps out and tells them what to do, but remember ‚Äúdon‚Äôt be cocky and demanding!‚Äù This is just like you are selling yourself and your plan to your teammates, and you can‚Äôt force them to buy your product. At this moment, they tend to cooperate because we all know that we have the same goal ‚Äî to complete the project with A.

However, toward the middle of the end of the meetings, there are tremendous works, such as midterm, finals, homework, and other projects, not to mention things that are outside the school. In this stage, people will be busy and are not able to dedicate all of themselves to the projects. This is actually predictable, but not many take action before it happens. The thing is that you should not put too many things to do around those busy weeks, so you will need to move some of them before or after those weeks, and this has to be done when you are making the initial plan. Moreover, at that moment, your teammates will sort of burn out. My point is that you need to be aware that these situations are always waiting for you, and oftentimes, it is inevitable.

As a result, you know that is coming, and you need to do something. I always do a little more setups for them to make their lives easier. For example, if someone will be working on user authentication using Spring-boot, then I will search and find useful tutorials for them to refer to. If someone wants to access our pi VM and implement the TFTP server, and then I will list out steps into bullet points for them. The more specific, the more they are willing to work on it. However, if you want to give them extremely specific steps, then it might take you too much time, and you would rather just finish them yourself. So I say this is so so so hard because you have to balance the tradeoff. Should I just do it myself, or should I teach them how to do it? But think about what you will learn throughout this process. If you only need to finish 50% of the project, and you lead and help others to finish the other 50%, then you will learn so much from just doing one project. And if you do this in three projects at the same time, you will be God.
